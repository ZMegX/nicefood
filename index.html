Nice, France

Nice, on France's southeast coast along the Mediterranean, has a rich and varied past shaped over ages by several civilisations. Originally established by the Greeks circa 350 BC and bearing Nikaia after the Greek goddess of victory, the city subsequently grew to be a major Roman colony. Having been part of the County of Savoy and subsequently the Kingdom of Sardinia before formally joining France in 1860, Nice was impacted during the Middle Ages by both French and Italian cultures.
While its moderate temperature and breathtaking waterfront, the Promenade des Anglais, drew European nobles in the 19th century, therefore establishing Nice as a top travel destination, the city's strategic maritime location made it an attractive place for trade and defence. Its mix of French and Italian influences, energetic old town (Vieux Nice), and historical sites today makes it an intriguing place to visit.
